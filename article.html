Prevalence of Antibiotic Resistance in Urban Clinics of Dhaka: A 2023 Study

SR
Dr. Sarah Rahman Dept. of Microbiology, Dhaka Medical College · Corresponding author
Oct 02, 2024 14 min read 3.2k views Share
"This study investigates the rising trend of antibiotic resistance among patients visiting urban clinics in Dhaka, by findings significant disparities between specific resistance patterns and resistance between specific antibiotic classes, highlighting an urgent need for revised antibiotic policy regimens in urban clinic areas."

Introduction

Antibiotic Resistance (ABR) poses one of the most significant threats to global health, food security, and healthcare sustainability today. In Bangladesh, growing population in densely packed urban centers like Dhaka, the disease and overuse of antibiotics are accelerating this crisis. This paper aims to describe the prevalence of antibiotic resistance (ABR) in outpatient settings, a sector often overlooked in favor of inpatient care in teaching hospitals.

The World Health Organisation (WHO) has classified ABR as a priority area for research. Multiple studies from neighboring nations across South Asia and South America, our attempt to construct a clearer picture of the current landscape.

Methodology

We conducted a cross-sectional study over a period of six months, from January to June 2023. A total of 1,230 clinical samples were collected from outpatient and inpatient hospital records. Identification of bacterial strains was performed using standard microbiological techniques, and the Kirby-Bauer disk diffusion method was used to determine susceptibility patterns.

Sample Size: 1,230 participants Age range 18-65 years, across 3 upazilas. At least one documented antibiotic prescription required for inclusion.
Eligibility Criteria: Urban patients only Being prescribed 18-65 years, at least one documented antibiotic prescription required. Patients with pre-existing immunodeficiency were excluded using a chi-square independence test.
Statistical Analysis: Chi-Square Test (p < 0.05) Significance tests were conducted using R version 4.2.1 with the tidyverse package ecosystem.

Results Analysis

Of the 1,230 samples, 785 (63.8%) tested positive for at least one resistant antibiotic. ESBL-producing bacteria constituted 46% of the total, followed by S. aureus (22%), and Rifampicin (AFR). Alarmingly, 44% of all positive samples were co-resistant to two or more antibiotic classes, and 9% faced pan-resistance.

Discussion

The high prevalence of ESBL-producing organisms has significant antibiotic management implications. The pattern suggests that antibiotic resistance genes are circulating within the community, not just in hospitals. This emphasizes the need for stronger surveillance between hospital and community-acquired infections. The unrestricted over-the-counter availability of antibiotics in local pharmacies clearly contributes significantly to this trend.

Conclusion

Our study recommends the critical need for mandatory surveillance legislation in healthcare settings urban to address ABR. Furthermore, community awareness solutions must be established in rural clinics to monitor resistance patterns effectively. Future research should check a direct focus on the genome-level characteristics of these resistant bacteria to track their transmission pathways.
